# Project overview
Indirect imaging of the magnetic reconnection region in flares using observations of chromospheric ribbon structure

Solar flares are explosive events on the Sun that drive massive ejections of plasma and have space weather impacts downstream at Earth. Magnetic fields on the Sun play a key role in these events, as regions of higher magnetic complexity become stressed and can undergo a process known as magnetic reconnection – a reconfiguring of the field, akin to snapping a twisted rubber band - releasing a massive amount of energy. We can observe signatures of this close to the surface of the Sun as bright ribbon-like structures; however, the crucial details of this energy release higher in the atmosphere are difficult to directly observe. The magnetic connectivity between the surface of the Sun and higher in the atmosphere allows us to indirectly observe this region, mapping evolution at the surface and undistorting it to infer details about this reconnection region and how it can drive space weather events and impacts. Work on this project will involve identifying candidate flaring events, understanding flare ribbon structure and evolution, and connecting with analysis tools to perform this imaging technique - all towards the goal of better understanding this region of magnetic reconnection.
